## Goal

Turn the premium personal AI cloud idea into a testable offer before building infrastructure or pitching it broadly.

## Working Hypothesis

Some premium clients may pay for a managed private AI office: personal cloud, controlled data, document memory, agents, multi-model AI, and individual services such as website, mail, and storage.

Core clarified product:

> protected private contour + correctly organized infrastructure + hardware/software tools + routine automation + workflows + AI in three roles: orchestrator, validator/assistant/secretary, and software developer.

Premium segment clarification from 2026-06-22:

> first validate people whose own attention and active context bandwidth have become the main constrained resource.

The product is premium because the pain appears when a person operates across many live context streams, not because the feature list is expensive.

Yuliya Malinina early feedback from 2026-06-22:

> top-manager work often looks like routine, but many escalations are actually uncertainty surfaces; AI should not be positioned as making strategic decisions for the top.

Validation consequence:

- test whether buyers want decision preparation and context reduction rather than decision replacement;
- ask what kinds of "small routine tasks" are actually unresolved rules, missing owners, unclear law, bank/client edge cases, or political approvals;
- define what the AI escalates to the human instead of pretending to automate.

## Expanded Thesis From Thread

User provided a longer thread where Roman Pekarskiy and Pavel Romanov reacted to the idea of agent-generated / disposable software.

Key validation constraints:

- do not sell only "fast app generation";
- test whether buyers feel pain around private context, domain knowledge, responsibility, verification, and support;
- account for the self-build objection: "why not just ask an agent to make my exact solution?";
- account for the enterprise objection: real systems have accumulated data, integrations, users, and compatibility constraints;
- treat review and responsibility as part of the offer, not as afterthoughts.

## Next Actions

1. Choose the first validation persona:
   - premium individual / founder;
   - small business owner;
   - consultant / expert team;
   - cloud-provider partner such as RTCloud.
2. Write a one-page offer with:
   - buyer pain;
   - concrete result in 14 days;
   - what is included;
   - what is explicitly not included;
   - privacy/security boundaries;
   - concrete security controls behind "protected private contour";
   - responsibility / verification boundary;
   - first owned domain model;
   - the primary AI role being sold first;
   - pilot price.
3. Decide whether the first version replaces SaaS tools or orchestrates existing Google/Yandex/Drive/mail surfaces.
4. Draft 5 outreach messages for people who could react to the offer.
5. Ask validators where the value sits:
   - private context / data;
   - domain model;
   - agent orchestration;
   - verification / audit;
   - managed support;
   - replacement of SaaS subscription.
6. Ask whether they already experience active context bandwidth as a bottleneck:
   - forgotten promises;
   - scattered relationship/project state;
   - stale decisions;
   - documents and follow-ups lost across tools;
   - repeated mental reconstruction of the working picture.
7. Ask where routine ends and uncertainty begins:
   - what must the AI prepare but not decide;
   - what should be escalated to the owner;
   - what information would make the owner's decision faster or calmer.
8. Ask which AI role is most legible and valuable:
   - orchestrator;
   - validator / assistant / secretary;
   - software developer.
9. Only after buyer-side validation, map RTCloud / infrastructure requirements.

## Do Not Do Yet

- Do not build a full private cloud stack.
- Do not promise medical, legal, or financial advice automation.
- Do not claim RTCloud partnership.
- Do not sell it as generic "AI assistant".
- Do not frame full product regeneration as safe for enterprise systems without addressing data, integrations, compatibility, users, and audit.
