## Raw Observations

Two patterns from user's experience:

1. A familiar person pulling the user toward a VTB role bragged that his calendar was fully packed, sometimes with two or three meetings at the same time. He said he "attends" all of them remotely, but does not listen.

2. In another enterprise meeting, eight people joined from the customer side; seven stayed silent the whole time.

These are strong examples of meeting theater:

- calendar load as status;
- presence as a substitute for participation;
- attendance without attention;
- quorum as crowd, not decision capacity;
- meetings as risk-distribution ritual rather than work.

## Core Thesis

Many organizations confuse attendance with involvement and meetings with decisions.

But a meeting where people are present but not listening is not coordination.

A meeting where seven out of eight people are silent is not alignment.

A calendar filled with simultaneous calls is not productivity. It is evidence that the organization has normalized attention fraud.

## Link To AI / Governance Theme

AI can summarize meetings, generate notes, extract action items and prepare follow-ups.

But AI cannot fix a meeting culture where:

- people attend without attention;
- no one owns the decision;
- participants are invited as political cover;
- meetings exist so everyone can say "I was involved";
- silence is treated as agreement;
- calendar density is mistaken for importance.

In such systems, AI may even make the theater more efficient:

- more meetings summarized;
- more action items generated;
- more notes distributed;
- more apparent progress;
- same absence of decision.

## Key Points

1. Calendar overload has become status.
   - being busy is mistaken for being needed;
   - simultaneous meetings are treated as normal;
   - attention becomes fake currency.

2. Attendance is not participation.
   - if you are connected but not listening, you are not in the meeting;
   - silent presence may be political cover;
   - silence is often not consent.

3. Meeting quorum is not headcount.
   - real quorum means people who can decide, approve, reject, commit resources or own risks;
   - eight listeners without authority are less useful than two owners with decision rights.

4. Meeting theater creates coordination debt.
   - decisions appear discussed but remain unowned;
   - absent attention creates later rework;
   - people who "were there" can still reopen questions later.

5. AI meeting tools can amplify the problem.
   - auto-summaries may create the illusion of alignment;
   - action items without ownership are just generated bureaucracy;
   - the problem is not note-taking, it is decision-making.

## Framework

Before scheduling a meeting, ask:

- What decision must be made?
- Who owns the decision?
- Who must be present because they can approve / reject / commit?
- Who only needs to be informed later?
- What evidence / artifact must be produced?
- What happens if the owner is absent?

Rules:

- Meeting without decision owner is discovery or status, not decision.
- Presence without attention does not count as participation.
- Silence should not count as approval unless explicitly agreed.
- People who do not attend the decision meeting should not reopen baseline without change-control.

## Final Thesis

The next productivity frontier is not fewer meeting notes.

It is fewer fake meetings.

AI can summarize discussion.

But only an organization can decide that attention, ownership and decision rights matter more than calendar theater.
