## Living Standard

- Rent for family-sized housing: high
- School options for two daughters: constrained and expensive
- Safety: acceptable in the common expat pockets, but still not a carefree default
- Healthcare: available, but quality and lived experience were not reassuring enough
- Education: expensive private path, crowded public path, limited comfort on availability

## Lived Experience Notes

This is not a theory note. You already lived there and left because the day-to-day stopped being sustainable.

- summer heat made sleeping without air conditioning essentially impossible;
- constant air conditioning added roughly EUR 500/month to running costs;
- even with good A/C, the environment led to recurring colds and respiratory issues;
- climate plus tourist turnover seemed to drive frequent viral infections that often became bacterial complications;
- both you and your wife were getting seriously ill roughly every three months, often with antibiotics and fever;
- the real monthly family burn you observed was much higher than the naive "EUR 1.5k/month" story;
- family housing, utilities, school/childcare, car costs, and fuel together pushed the lived cost much closer to a heavy-relocation lifestyle than a cheap one.

## Relocation Economics

- One-time relocation costs: high
- Monthly cost delta vs Novosibirsk: high
- Company support available: must be strong to justify the move
- Relocation without company support: possible only if income is clearly above the family burn rate
- Tax / admin notes: tax base and net income matter a lot; raw salary alone is not enough

## Hiring Filter Notes

- When the country is a green flag: roles where relocation support is real and the package is strong enough to justify the move.
- When the country is a yellow flag: if the role is strong but family logistics need checking.
- When the country is a red flag: if the package is thin, if the climate will drive health problems, or if the move would force a bad school/housing compromise.

## Decision Rule

- Cyprus is not a blanket no;
- Cyprus is a boundary-case country that needs an unusually strong package to win back the lived-life friction you already experienced;
- if another country gives similar compensation with less climate and health pain, Cyprus should lose.
